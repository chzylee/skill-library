# Research grounding — why content-starter's patterns work

The coach's patterns (funnel stages, mode biases, shapes) are not generic tips — each
encodes a researched mechanism of what actually holds attention and persuades. This file
is the checkable grounding so the skill behaves as a *reviewed advisor*. **The coach does
not cite these at runtime**; the research shapes its judgment, not its output.

Honesty about evidence strength is part of the point: the claim is "each pattern traces to
a source you can check," **not** "every pattern is proven." Strength is flagged per row.

| Pattern in the skill | Mechanism it applies | Primary source | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| The funnel (Hook → Draw-in → Hold → Ender) | Attention → interest → desire → action progression | E. St. Elmo Lewis, AIDA (1898); codified by Strong (1925) | Historical / foundational |
| Hook — win the first seconds | Most short-form drop-off is in the first ~3s; leading with the payoff (not branding) lifts retention | *Decoding the Hook* (arXiv 2026); platform retention data | Practitioner + emerging CS |
| Draw-in / Hold — open one curiosity gap | Curiosity is felt deprivation from a perceived knowledge gap; an opened gap drives resolution-seeking | Loewenstein (1994), *Psychological Bulletin* 116:75–98; Zeigarnik (1927) | Peer-reviewed (Zeigarnik memory effect replicates weakly) |
| Hold — concrete example over abstract claim | Concrete language is recalled better, judged more truthful, shared and bought more | *J. Consumer Research* 47:787 (2020); concrete-language sharing studies (2025) | Peer-reviewed |
| Story shape — build an absorbing arc | "Transportation" (absorption into narrative) increases story-consistent beliefs and favorable evaluations | Green & Brock (2000), *JPSP* 79:701–721 | Peer-reviewed |
| Growth mode — optimize for reach | Growth comes from reaching many light buyers via mental availability, not deepening a loyal few | Byron Sharp, *How Brands Grow* (2010), Ehrenberg-Bass | Evidence-based book |
| Brand mode — optimize for identity over time | Brand-building (long-term, emotional) is a different job from activation; ~60/40 maximizes total effect | Binet & Field, *The Long and the Short of It* (2013), IPA | Meta-analytic (IPA Databank, ~1,000 cases) |
| Community mode — optimize for relationship | Audiences form one-sided but real-feeling ("parasocial") bonds with a persona | Horton & Wohl (1956), *Psychiatry* 19:215–229 | Peer-reviewed (foundational) |
| The Agency Law | Opinionated AI writing assistants shift what users write *and think*; AI can steer people from their values | Jakesch et al. (2023), CHI; Nishal, Lee et al. (2026), CHI | Peer-reviewed |

## Notes that change how to apply a pattern
- **Hook (first seconds):** the strongest numbers are practitioner/platform data plus
  nascent computational work — treat "first 3 seconds" as a well-supported heuristic, not
  a law. Front-load the concrete payoff; don't over-claim.
- **Open loop:** the *curiosity-gap* mechanism (Loewenstein) is the sturdy citation; the
  Zeigarnik *memory* effect replicates weakly, so use **one** open loop and don't rely on
  it as the whole retention strategy.
- **Growth vs. Brand are separate modes on purpose:** Sharp (reach/penetration) and
  Binet & Field (brand-building vs. activation) genuinely disagree at the edges. Keeping
  them as distinct, user-chosen modes is the honest move — the user picks which bet.
- **Agency Law:** Jakesch et al. is direct experimental evidence (1,506 participants) that
  a persistent tool bias reshapes the user; that's the empirical reason bias must be
  user-chosen, named when it acts, and never allowed to override stated values.
